Case Study 01 · Supply Chain AI

Kinaxis Maestro AI Supply Chain Pilot

FMCG / Beverages Kinaxis Maestro AI Malaysia → China (APAC Lead) Apr 2022 – Sep 2023 Carlsberg APAC
The Problem

Carlsberg operates in 150 markets — each with its own ERP configuration, data structures, and supply chain planning workflows. Deploying Kinaxis Maestro AI, which requires clean, consistent, well-integrated data, turned that fragmentation into a critical delivery risk. Malaysia was selected as the APAC pilot: high motivation, real stakes, global visibility, and China already positioned as the next market in the queue.

What I Did · Regional Deployment Lead for Asia

I came in focused on the data first — not the technology. Every source system feeding Kinaxis needed validation, gap identification, and remediation sequencing. By go-live, the team was closing known issues, not discovering new ones. The go-live itself ran as a structured command center operation: real-time issue triage, defined escalation paths, and cross-functional coordination across global leadership, regional IT, supply chain operations, and the Kinaxis vendor — in English, Cantonese, and Mandarin as the conversation required.

In parallel, I coordinated a significant China data sovereignty risk: food-related data classified as sensitive, with AI servers sitting outside China's borders. I worked between Kinaxis and Carlsberg's security team to give Deloitte the technical clarity needed for a legal recommendation. The matter was unresolved when I left — but China subsequently went live on Kinaxis, confirming the path was viable.

What Made It Hard
  • Multiple ERP and planning systems, each requiring individual data validation — managed through a shared tracker replacing informal status with structured milestone accountability.
  • Fixed APAC go-live sequenced within a global rollout. Front-loading risk identification meant when the timeline compressed, we were finishing work, not starting it.
  • Three organizations, multiple time zones, different priorities — Carlsberg global, Malaysian regional ops, and Kinaxis vendor all aligned through a single source of truth for status, decisions, and escalations.
Outcome

Malaysia went live on schedule — a production-ready AI supply chain planning environment integrated into Carlsberg's regional operations. The delivery directly accelerated China's positioning as the next global rollout market. China subsequently went live, confirming both the coordination model and the data sovereignty path were replicable.

Key Learning

The most important lesson had nothing to do with the technology. It was about the politics of global program governance: when the pilot market team can only answer for the pilot market, questions with global template implications must go back to global. If that boundary isn't held, pilot preferences quietly become global design decisions — and the next market carries the blame for choices that were never theirs to make.

Case Study 02 · Cloud AI Infrastructure

GCP AI Health Analytics Foundation

Retail / Health & Beauty Google Cloud Platform (GCP) Hong Kong Late 2025 – Early 2026 DFI Retail Group / Mannings
The Problem

DFI Retail Group operates over 11,000 outlets across 13 countries. Mannings, its health and beauty chain, had an ambitious AI vision: personalized recommendations, biometric wellness assessments, omnichannel integration. The infrastructure to support it didn't exist. Before any AI model could run, a working GCP cloud foundation needed to be established — servers stood up, vendors engaged, cross-functional teams aligned.

What I Did · Parallel Program Coordinator

I was already on a higher-priority group-level recovery program when the HK IT head asked if I could help in parallel. I agreed — the work was genuinely interesting: an AI foundation program in a health data environment, exactly the kind of engagement I wanted more experience in.

My role was coordination: structured meetings across business, IT, data, infrastructure, cloud, and vendor teams — multiple workstreams moving simultaneously under a tight timeframe. Every meeting had a clear agenda. Every session produced documented actions and post-meeting highlights. Every action got chased. The program operated in a PII and PHI-sensitive environment where that shared awareness shaped every coordination decision.

After approximately two months, I recognized risk building on the higher-priority recovery program. I made the professional call to re-prioritize — transitioning the Mannings coordination cleanly so the team could continue without losing momentum.

Outcome

GCP servers stood up and ready for the build team to commence. Vendor relationships established. Cross-functional teams aligned. Integration dependencies mapped. The teams inheriting the program received a complete picture — documented actions and clear handover notes, not an optimistic verbal summary.

Key Learning

The HK IT head brought me in expecting technical engagement beyond typical PM coordination — cloud infrastructure concepts, GCP architecture, vocabulary that enables a real conversation rather than just facilitating one. I could coordinate effectively, but I felt the gap. That experience directly inspired the Google Cloud ADP certification I passed in January 2026: not just as a credential, but to build the working technical vocabulary that AI and data projects increasingly require from the people managing them.

Case Study 03 · ERP Modernization

Global D365 ERP Template — Carlsberg Asia

FMCG / Beverages Microsoft Dynamics 365 China Pilot → Malaysia & Singapore Apr 2022 – Sep 2023 Carlsberg Asia
The Problem

Every Carlsberg market had grown its own ERP configuration over time — different implementations, localized workflows, inconsistent data standards. The answer was a global D365 standardization program: one platform, one template. China was chosen as the Asian pilot — not despite being the most complex market, but because of it. If the template works for China, it works for every market that follows.

What I Did · Regional PM for Asia (PMO Level)

My role was to manage the managers — not tasks. Three local PMs were on the ground in China. My job was regional alignment, keeping global leadership informed, and ensuring Asian market requirements were fully captured in the global template before design decisions accelerated.

The core of this was a structured requirements exercise: 1,154 Level 3 requirements collected from China, then validated across every other Asian market in a cross-market survey — which requirements were China-specific, which were regionally common, which could be resolved through configuration. That picture went to global for approval, and from it came both the China solution and the foundation of the global template. I also co-led the RFP process for post-China Asia implementation partners, with criteria explicitly weighted to surface genuine regional delivery experience alongside global capability.

Outcome

The China pilot delivered a validated global D365 template that became the foundation for Malaysia and Singapore. Markets inheriting the template started from a decision record — pre-resolved compliance considerations, documented requirements framework, substantially reduced discovery cycles. Malaysia subsequently became the Kinaxis AI pilot. The D365 standardization and the AI supply chain deployment are connected, not coincidental: clean ERP data is what makes AI supply chain planning reliable.

Key Learning

Three convictions from this program: start with the hardest market — if the template works for China's full complexity, every simpler market is already solved. ERP platform skills are transferable — D365 and SAP differ but the PM discipline is identical. And governance shows up when scrutiny arrives — when Internal Audit came to review, my job was simple: know where everything is, get them what they need quickly. No scrambling, no gaps, no surprises.

Perspective

Reflections & Key Learnings

I don't think what I've done constitutes a "pivot" into AI. The Carlsberg Kinaxis deployment, the DFI Mannings infrastructure program, the D365 template work — they all required the same underlying discipline as the ERP recoveries and cloud migrations before them. What's changed is that the technology now makes decisions, not just processes. That changes the stakes around governance. It doesn't change what governance is.

01

Governance is the delivery mechanism, not a constraint on it.

Programs that treat data quality, risk identification, and model alignment as parallel workstreams rather than preconditions discover their structural problems under go-live pressure — when the cost of fixing them is highest. You can't govern your way out of a live production crisis. You prevent it.

02

AI handles the happy path. Humans handle everything else.

Enterprise AI executes standard cases well. But real operations are full of exceptions nobody anticipated when the system was designed. Those are where AI breaks down and human judgment steps in — not just to fix the immediate problem, but to refine the system and close the gaps. In enterprise AI programs, humans aren't just oversight. They're the ongoing improvement mechanism.

03

APAC is not a geography. It's a set of operating conditions.

Multilingual environments, regulatory variation across markets, the gap between what HQ wants and what regional teams can absorb — these aren't complications. They're the standard. Programs that plan for idealized conditions fail. Programs that plan for APAC conditions deliver.

04

ERP standardization is AI readiness.

The organizations asking "when do we start the AI program?" are often already building it when they clean up their ERP data. Clean data is what makes AI useful. And AI may finally solve ERP's oldest complaint — usability. If users can simply tell the system what they need in plain language, companies may no longer need to customize ERP for user convenience. A clean standard template, AI handling the user experience layer — less customization, cleaner data, better AI outputs. A virtuous cycle.

05

Recovery experience makes you a better first-pass leader.

Having rebuilt programs under pressure teaches you exactly how they fail. I structure preventively for the failure modes I've seen most often: unclear accountability, late risk identification, governance treated as overhead. The result is that the programs I lead don't need recovering.
